### 1. Featured Snippet Definition Box

**What is the Anatomy of a Roof?**
A roof system consists of multiple layers: the structural deck, underlayment, flashing at penetrations and edges, shingles (or other covering), ventilation components, and drainage systems. Each layer serves a specific purpose in protecting your home from water, wind, and weather.

### 3. Detailed Component Breakdown

**STRUCTURAL LAYER: The Roof Deck**

"The deck is what everything else sits on. Here's what I see in Charlotte:

- **Plywood (CDX):** Most common in newer homes. 1/2" or 5/8" thick.
- **OSB (Oriented Strand Board):** More affordable, performs well when dry. Common since 1990s.
- **Tongue and Groove Boards:** Found in older Charlotte homes (pre-1960s).

**Charlotte Note:** I see a lot of OSB rot in homes where soffit vents were blocked. Moisture builds up and OSB swells. If your attic feels humid, check your ventilation before it damages your deck."

---

**PROTECTION LAYER 1: Underlayment**

"Underlayment is your backup waterproofing. If water gets past your shingles, this is what saves your deck.

- **Felt (15# or 30#):** Traditional choice. Works, but can tear during installation.
- **Synthetic Underlayment:** What we use. Stronger, doesn't tear, lies flatter. Costs more but worth it.
- **Self-Adhering (Peel and Stick):** Used at high-risk areas like valleys and eaves.

**2026 Code Requirement:** Ice and water shield required at eaves (first 24" minimum) and in all valleys per NC building code."

---

**PROTECTION LAYER 2: Flashing**

"Flashing is where most roof leaks happen. It seals every joint and penetration.

- **Step Flashing:** L-shaped metal pieces woven into shingles where roof meets wall
- **Counter Flashing:** Covers top of step flashing, embedded in mortar joints
- **Valley Flashing:** Metal channel in roof valleys (if using open valley method)
- **Drip Edge:** Metal at roof edges that directs water into gutters
- **Pipe Boots:** Rubber seals around plumbing vents

**What I See Wrong in Charlotte:**
Most leak calls I get are flashing failures, not shingle failures. Common problems:
- Counter flashing not embedded in mortar (just caulked to brick)
- Step flashing too short (should extend 4" minimum under shingles)
- Rusted galvanized flashing (should use aluminum in our climate)
- Dried-out pipe boots (UV destroys rubber in 12-15 years)"

---

**COVERING LAYER: Shingles**

"The shingles are what you see, but they're just one part of the system.

**Shingle Types We Install in Charlotte:**

| Type | Lifespan | Cost/SF | Best For |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| 3-Tab | 15-20 years | $5.50-$7.00 | Budget, rental properties |
| Architectural | 25-30 years | $7.00-$9.50 | Most Charlotte homes |
| Premium | 30-40 years | $9.50-$12.00 | High-end homes, resale value |
| Designer | 40-50 years | $12.00-$15.00+ | Historic look, statement homes |

**Shingle Components:**
- Fiberglass mat (core strength)
- Asphalt coating (waterproofing)
- Ceramic granules (UV protection, color)
- Sealant strip (wind resistance)

**Specialty Shingles:**
- Starter strip: First row with sealant positioned correctly
- Hip and ridge caps: Thicker, designed for bending over peaks
- Architectural vs 3-tab: Architectural has random cutout pattern for dimensional look"

---

**VENTILATION COMPONENTS**

"Ventilation is the most overlooked part of roof anatomy. Bad ventilation kills roofs early.

**Intake Vents (Bring Air In):**
- Soffit vents: In eave overhangs
- Edge vents: Under drip edge
- Required: 1 SF per 150 SF of attic space

**Exhaust Vents (Let Air Out):**
- Ridge vent: Along entire ridge, most effective
- Box/turtle vents: Individual units, visible from ground
- Power vents: Fan-assisted, uses electricity
- Gable vents: In gable walls, least effective for modern homes

**Charlotte Ventilation Math:**
For a 1,500 SF attic:
- Required ventilation: 10 SF total
- 50% intake (5 SF at soffits)
- 50% exhaust (5 SF at ridge)

**Common Charlotte Problem:**
Blown-in insulation blocking soffit vents. When I inspect attics and feel hot, humid air with no airflow at the eaves, this is usually why."

---

**DRAINAGE: Gutters and Downspouts**

"Technically not 'on' the roof, but essential to the system.

- **Gutters:** 5" standard, 6" for steep roofs or heavy rain areas
- **Downspouts:** One per 20-25 linear feet of gutter
- **Extensions:** Should discharge 4-6 feet from foundation
- **Gutter Guards:** Optional but helpful with Charlotte's tree coverage

**Sizing for Charlotte:**
Our rainfall (43" annual average) requires properly sized gutters. Undersized gutters overflow, causing fascia rot and foundation issues."

### 4. How Components Work Together

"Think of your roof as a layered defense system:

**First Defense:** Shingles - shed water off the surface
**Second Defense:** Underlayment - catches anything that gets through
**Third Defense:** Flashing - seals all the weak points (penetrations, joints)
**Fourth Defense:** Deck - structural barrier
**Support System:** Ventilation - prevents moisture buildup that weakens everything

When one layer fails, the others compensate - but not forever. That's why we inspect the whole system, not just what you can see."

### 5. What Homeowners Should Know

**For Inspections:**
- Know where your penetrations are (chimneys, vents, skylights)
- Understand your ventilation setup
- Know your shingle type and approximate age

**For Getting Quotes:**
- Ask what underlayment they use (synthetic > felt)
- Ask about ice and water shield at eaves
- Verify they'll replace flashing, not just re-use old
- Ask about starter strip and ridge cap brands

**For Maintenance:**
- Clean gutters twice yearly
- Check pipe boots every 10 years
- Keep tree limbs 10' from roof
- Clear debris from valleys

### 6. FAQ Section

**Q: What are the basic parts of a roof?**
A: A roof consists of: the structural deck, underlayment, ice & water shield at vulnerable areas, drip edge at perimeters, flashing at penetrations and walls, shingles, ridge and hip caps, ventilation components, and the gutter/drainage system.

**Q: What is the purpose of roof underlayment?**
A: Underlayment is a water-resistant or waterproof barrier between your roof deck and shingles. It provides backup protection if water penetrates past the shingles and protects the deck during installation.

**Q: What is roof flashing and why is it important?**
A: Flashing is metal (usually aluminum) that seals joints, penetrations, and transitions on your roof. Most roof leaks occur at flashing points - around chimneys, where roof meets wall, in valleys, and around vents.

**Q: How do I know if my roof ventilation is adequate?**
A: Signs of poor ventilation: attic over 120°F in summer, moisture/frost on underside of deck in winter, premature shingle aging, ice dams. NC code requires 1 SF of ventilation per 150 SF of attic.

**Q: What's the difference between ridge vent and box vents?**
A: Ridge vents run continuously along the peak, providing even ventilation across the entire attic. Box vents are individual units that ventilate specific areas. Ridge vents are generally more effective and less visible.

**Q: How long do different roof components last?**
A: Shingles: 20-30 years. Underlayment: same as shingles. Flashing: 25-30 years (longer if aluminum). Pipe boots: 12-15 years. Deck: 30-50+ years (unless water damaged). Ridge vents: 20-25 years.
