### 1. Featured Snippet Definition Box

**What is a Roof Ridge?**
The roof ridge is the horizontal peak where two sloped roof sections meet at the top. It's the highest point of your roof and typically covered by ridge cap shingles or a ridge vent for attic ventilation.

**What is a Roof Valley?**
A roof valley is the V-shaped channel where two sloped roof sections meet and slope downward. Valleys funnel water toward gutters and are one of the most leak-prone areas on any roof.

### 3. Open Valley vs Closed Valley Comparison

**Open Valley Method:**
- Metal flashing visible in valley
- Water runs directly on metal
- Easier debris clearing
- Longer lifespan (20-30 years)
- Higher material cost
- Best for: Heavy tree coverage, steep slopes

**Closed Valley Method (Woven or Cut):**
- Shingles cover the valley
- Cleaner appearance
- Shingles wear faster in valley
- More prone to debris trapping
- Lower material cost
- Best for: Minimal trees, aesthetic priority

**My Recommendation for Charlotte:**
"For most Charlotte homes, I recommend open metal valleys. With our tree coverage and the debris from summer storms, you want water to run clean without shingle overlap trapping leaves and twigs. Yes, you can see the metal, but it's going to last longer and leak less."

### 4. Ridge Vent vs Box Vent Section

**Ridge Vents:**
- Continuous ventilation along entire ridge
- Clean appearance - almost invisible
- Better airflow per linear foot
- Cost: $3-5 per linear foot installed
- Best for: Most Charlotte homes

**Box/Turtle Vents:**
- Individual vents spaced along roof
- More visible from ground
- Can be blocked by debris/snow
- Cost: $50-75 each installed
- Best for: Supplemental ventilation, hip roofs

**2026 Charlotte Ventilation Code:**
NC requires 1 square foot of ventilation per 150 square feet of attic space. For a typical 1,500 SF attic, that's 10 SF of total ventilation (split between intake at soffits and exhaust at ridge).

### 5. Maintenance Checklist

**Annual Ridge Inspection (Do Every Spring):**
- [ ] Check ridge cap shingles for cracks or lifting
- [ ] Look for gaps in ridge vent
- [ ] Verify ridge cap shingles are sealed
- [ ] Check hip ridges for the same issues

**Annual Valley Inspection:**
- [ ] Clear all debris from valleys
- [ ] Check for rust on metal flashing (open valleys)
- [ ] Look for wear patterns on shingles (closed valleys)
- [ ] Inspect valley at chimney/wall intersections
- [ ] Check for water stains in attic below valleys

**After Every Major Storm:**
- [ ] Visual check from ground for missing ridge caps
- [ ] Look for debris accumulation in valleys
- [ ] Check for lifted or shifted shingles at ridges

### 7. FAQ Section

**Q: What is a roof ridge and why is it important?**
A: The roof ridge is the horizontal peak where two roof slopes meet. It's critical for structural integrity and ventilation. Ridge cap shingles or ridge vents protect this vulnerable joint from water intrusion.

**Q: How often should roof valleys be inspected?**
A: Inspect valleys twice yearly (spring and fall) and after any major storm. In Charlotte, our tree coverage means valleys accumulate debris quickly - clear them during gutter cleaning.

**Q: What causes roof valley leaks?**
A: Valley leaks typically result from: worn shingles in closed valleys, corroded flashing in open valleys, debris dams blocking water flow, improper installation, or ice dams (rare in Charlotte but possible).

**Q: Should I choose open or closed valleys for my Charlotte home?**
A: For most Charlotte homes, open metal valleys perform better. Our heavy tree coverage and debris from storms means open valleys stay cleaner and last longer. Closed valleys look cleaner but require more maintenance.

**Q: How long do ridge cap shingles last?**
A: Ridge caps typically last 12-15 years - shorter than field shingles because they're more exposed to sun, wind, and weather. Budget for ridge cap replacement midway through your roof's life.

**Q: Can I walk on my roof to inspect ridges and valleys?**
A: I don't recommend it. Steep ridges and wet valleys are slip hazards, and walking on shingles can cause damage. Use binoculars from the ground or hire a professional inspection.
